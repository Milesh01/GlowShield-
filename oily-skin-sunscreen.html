Buying Guide

What to Look For in a
Sunscreen for Oily Skin

🧪
Formula Type

For oily skin, choose gel-based or water-based formulas. Avoid cream or balm textures. Chemical sunscreens generally feel lighter than mineral ones, though newer micronized mineral formulas are catching up fast.

🌿
Key Ingredients

Look for niacinamide (regulates sebum), salicylic acid (prevents breakouts), and silica (absorbs oil). Avoid alcohol denat as the first ingredient — it can over-dry and cause rebound oiliness.

🏷️
Labels to Seek

Always look for non-comedogenic, oil-free, and broad-spectrum on the packaging. "Matte finish" is a bonus signal. "PA++++" rating (Asian sunscreens) indicates superior UVA protection.

☀️
SPF Number

SPF 30 is the dermatologist minimum for daily use. SPF 50 for extended outdoors. Consistent reapplication every 2 hours matters more than chasing SPF 100 — a higher number gives diminishing returns.

Common Questions

Everything about sunscreen for oily and acne-prone skin answered.

Can sunscreen make oily skin worse or cause breakouts?
+
Yes — the wrong sunscreen absolutely can. Creamy, oil-rich formulas or those with comedogenic ingredients like coconut oil will clog pores and worsen breakouts. The solution is to only use sunscreens labeled non-comedogenic and oil-free. All 7 picks in this guide pass that test. If you experience breakouts, it's usually the formula type, not sunscreen itself.
Should I use mineral or chemical sunscreen for oily skin?
+
Both can work, but chemical sunscreens typically feel lighter and leave less white cast — making them the preferred choice for oily skin. Mineral sunscreens (zinc oxide/titanium dioxide) are better for sensitive or acne-inflamed skin. The Beauty of Joseon and Isntree picks on this list are excellent chemical options with no white cast at all.
How do I stop my sunscreen from making my face shiny?
+
Choose matte-finish formulas (La Roche-Posay Anthelios, EltaMD UV Clear). Apply a translucent setting powder or SPF powder spray on top. Avoid layering thick moisturizer underneath — a lightweight toner or serum is enough for oily skin. And reapply with a powder SPF rather than a lotion to avoid buildup.
How much sunscreen should I apply on my face?
+
The standard recommendation is a nickel-sized amount (about 1/4 teaspoon or 1.5ml) for just the face and neck. Most people use 25–50% of what's needed to reach the labeled SPF. Under-application is the #1 reason sunscreen doesn't work as expected — don't be shy with it.
Do I still need sunscreen if my foundation has SPF?
+
Yes — always apply a dedicated sunscreen underneath. Foundation SPF is never applied in a thick enough layer to reach its labeled protection. Think of foundation SPF as a small bonus, not your primary protection. Your dedicated SPF is your real defense.
